What’s Superposition?

Quantum mechanics is pretty wild. Particles like electrons don’t just sit quietly in one state, waiting for you to check on them. Until you actually measure them, they exist in a kind of limbo—a mix of all their possible states at once. Physicists call this a wavefunction. It’s basically a list of every outcome that could happen, along with how likely each one is.

In my simulation, the quantumStates array works like that wavefunction. As long as the box stays closed, the zombie doesn’t pick a single state. It’s all of them at once—normal, doubled, dancing, or totally gone. That little ❓ stands for the mystery.

So, while the box is shut, here’s what’s going on:

    • What Happens When You Open the Box?

    Cracking open the box is like making a measurement in quantum physics. The wavefunction collapses, and the zombie snaps into just one of those states—the rest vanish. But here’s the weird part: it’s not like flipping a coin, where the answer was already there and you just didn’t know it. The zombie genuinely wasn’t in any specific state until you looked. That’s what makes quantum mechanics so strange.

    Which state do you get? That’s down to the Born Rule: the chance of each outcome is its amplitude squared. In my simulation, all four possibilities are equally likely—so each has a 25% shot. That’s why I use Math.random(). The randomness isn’t just ignorance; it’s baked right into nature.

    How My Simulation Matches Real Quantum Physics

    In the real world, quantum particles don’t have fixed traits until you measure them. Their wavefunction holds all the possible outcomes and their odds. In my game, the zombie stands in for a quantum particle, and the quantumStates array is its wavefunction. When you open the box, you force the zombie into one definite state—just like a real quantum measurement.

    Of course, real quantum systems are even touchier. Superposition is super fragile—just bumping into something else can collapse it. That’s decoherence, and it’s why you never see your keys or your cat in two places at once. Schrödinger’s cat was meant to show how odd things would get if quantum rules worked on big stuff.

    Entanglement: Two Zombies, One Fate

    Entanglement is another crazy quantum effect. When two particles get tangled up, you can’t talk about one without the other. Instead of each having its own wavefunction, they share. So until you measure them, they’re stuck in this joint superposition. Measure one, and you instantly know what happened to the other—even if they’re far apart.

    In my game, I show this with a second zombie box. Open one box, and you immediately know what state the other zombie’s in. Their fates are tied together.

    Tunneling: When Zombies Disappear

    Quantum tunneling is when a tiny particle slips through a barrier it shouldn’t be able to cross. Imagine a ball that doesn’t have enough energy to roll over a hill. In normal physics, it’s stuck. But in quantum mechanics, there’s a small chance it pops up on the other side, as if it sneaked through the hill.

    That’s what the 👻 disappeared zombie shows. When you open the box and the zombie’s gone, its probability wave slipped through the walls and tunneled out. Poof—no more zombie inside.

    Want to see tunneling in action? Try the “Try to Escape” button while the box is closed. There’s a 20% chance the zombie sneaks out before you even open it. That’s tunneling happening without you looking.
